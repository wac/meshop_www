Indirect Gene – Disease Association via Medical Subject Term Annotation of Literature Evidence
Warren A. Cheung1,2, B.F. Francis Ouellette3, Wyeth W. Wasserman1,4
1 Centre for Molecular Medicine and Therapeutics, University of British Columbia
2 Bioinformatics Program, University of British Columbia
3 Ontario Institute for Cancer Research, Toronto, Ontario, Canada
4 Department of Medical Genetics, UBC

The ever-expanding set of known interconnections between biomedical databases now makes it possible for the computational prediction of novel gene-disease linkages. Shown here is the use of interconnections in biomedical literature to prioritise human genes playing a previously unknown functional role in the pathology of one or more diseases, specifically in experiments yielding large lists of genes such as genome-wide association studies via SNP arrays and sequencing methods. Novel linkages are identified by identifying direct literature association profiles of genes highly similar to the direct literature association profiles of diseases. Several scoring methodologies have been evaluated for the ability to predict novel associations between gene and disease using resources from 2007 to 2009. Evaluation has also been performed against curated reference sources for gene-disease relationships. The utility of our method is demonstrated in several examples of genome-wide association studies.
